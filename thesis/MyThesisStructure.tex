\begin{abstract}

The correctness and quality of pharmacovigilance data are of high importance, as adverse event information from sources like the FDA's Adverse Event Reporting System (FAERS) is often fragmented, which prevents a full analysis of drug safety profiles and inhibits the correlation of real-world adverse events with a drug's regulatory status.
This thesis aims to address this data integration challenge by following a Design Science methodology, in order to develop a domain-specific ontology. In the course of this thesis, data for six common analgesics were programmatically extracted from the openFDA API and subsequently used to populate a knowledge graph, a representation that integrates patient demographics with U.S. regulatory information.
An evaluation of the resulting knowledge graph is performed using SPARQL queries to assess its analytical capabilities. As a result, the analysis reveals a "risk-regulation paradox," showing that the legal classification of a drug does not consistently align with its empirical safety data; for instance, the over-the-counter drug Paracetamol presented a higher percentage of fatal outcomes in the analyzed dataset than the prescription drug Naproxen.
Finally, a data-driven framework is presented to show the potential of the resulting knowledge graph, which serves as a proof-of-concept for applying semantic technologies, to democratize access to drug safety data, while also empowering patients and policymakers with a transparent as well as independent tool for risk assessment. Ultimately, this work contributes to a foundation for finding objective clarity on complex public health issues. 
\end{abstract}
